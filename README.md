# All-Action
I spent some time thinking about this properly. What you’re describing is essentially the Oxford History of Gambling — a work that functions simultaneously as narrative history, cultural encyclopedia, and reference volume. Here’s my attempt at designing that book. It’s long, because the subject demands it.

ALL ACTION: A Complete History and Encyclopedia of Gambling
In Five Books

BOOK ONE: FOUNDATIONS
The origins, anthropology, mathematics, and moral philosophy of gambling from prehistory through the Enlightenment

PART I: BEFORE THE BET
Chapter 1 — The Gambling Animal
Why humans gamble. Evolutionary psychology of risk-seeking behavior. The neuroscience of anticipation, dopamine, and variable-ratio reinforcement. Comparison with risk-taking behavior in other species. The argument that gambling is not a bug but a feature of human cognition — a training ground for decision-making under uncertainty.
Chapter 2 — Bones and Lots: The Archaeological Record
The oldest known gambling artifacts. Astragali (knucklebones) found in sites dating to 40,000 BCE. Dice in Mesopotamian tombs. Casting lots in ancient societies worldwide. The material culture of early gambling — what survives, what we can infer, and what we’re guessing about.
Chapter 3 — Divination and Its Shadow
The deep entanglement between gambling and religion in the ancient world. Casting lots to determine the will of the gods. Cleromancy, sortition, and the sacred random. The argument that gambling began as a religious act and only later became secular entertainment. How the same knucklebone could be a holy oracle or a street hustle depending on context.
Chapter 4 — Games of the First Civilizations
The Royal Game of Ur. Senet in Egypt — a game with cosmological meaning. The Indian game of Pachisi and its descendants. The Chinese game of Liubo. Each treated as a case study in how early civilizations embedded gambling into their broader culture. What the rules (where known) tell us about how these societies understood chance.

PART II: THE CLASSICAL AND ANCIENT WORLD
Chapter 5 — Greece: Chance and Fate
Gambling in Homer. Dice games and their social context. The Greek attitude toward tyche (fortune) and how it shaped tolerance for gambling. Plato and Aristotle on games of chance. The philosophical tension between rationality and randomness.
Chapter 6 — Rome: The Republic Bets
Rome as perhaps the most gambling-obsessed society in the ancient world. Dice (tesserae and tali), chariot racing and the circus factions, gladiatorial betting. The Lex Alearia and other anti-gambling laws — and their spectacular failure. Gambling as a marker of Roman social life from emperor to slave. Augustus, Nero, and Claudius as famous degenerate gamblers. Tabernae and the physical spaces of Roman gambling.
Chapter 7 — India: Dice, Dharma, and the Mahabharata
The Rig Veda‘s Gambler’s Hymn — possibly the oldest literary treatment of gambling addiction. The dice game in the Mahabharata as civilizational narrative. Vibhitaka nuts as dice. The Arthashastra’s remarkably modern regulatory framework for gambling houses. Gambling in Hindu, Jain, and Buddhist ethical frameworks. The long tradition of Diwali gambling.
Chapter 8 — China: From Liubo to Mah-Jongg
Gambling in Chinese civilization from the Shang Dynasty forward. Cockfighting, cricket fighting, and animal contests. The invention of playing cards (the yezi ge or “leaf game”) during the Tang Dynasty. Tile games and their evolution. Pai Gow. Fan-Tan. The Chinese lottery (baige piao) and its enormous cultural footprint. Imperial attempts at prohibition and why they repeatedly failed.
Chapter 9 — Japan: Gambling in the Floating World
Bakuchi and tōbaku — Japanese gambling culture from the Heian period. Dice games, card games (karuta, hanafuda), and the tekiya and bakuto — itinerant gamblers who became the origin of the Yakuza. Gambling in Edo-period popular culture. Sumo and horse-racing wagering.
Chapter 10 — The Pre-Columbian Americas and the Pacific
Gambling among Indigenous peoples of North America — the hand game, stick games, hoop and pole, and the enormous social and spiritual significance of wagering. The Aztec game of patolli and its cosmological dimensions. Polynesian gambling traditions. The argument that gambling existed in virtually every human society ever documented.
Chapter 11 — The Islamic World: Prohibition, Practice, and Paradox
Maysir and qimar — gambling in the Qur’an and Hadith. The explicit prohibition and its theological basis. How Muslim societies nonetheless developed rich gambling cultures, particularly around horse racing, pigeon racing, and backgammon (tawla). The legal and philosophical distinctions drawn between games of skill and games of chance. Ottoman coffee houses as gambling venues.

PART III: THE MATHEMATICS OF CHANCE
Chapter 12 — Thousands of Years Without Probability
The great intellectual mystery: why did no ancient civilization develop a mathematical theory of chance despite centuries of gambling? The Greek near-miss. Possible explanations — theological, philosophical, and practical. What it means that humanity gambled for millennia with no formal understanding of odds.
Chapter 13 — Cardano’s Liber de Ludo Aleae
Girolamo Cardano — physician, mathematician, compulsive gambler — and his 16th-century manuscript on games of chance. The first systematic attempt to quantify probability. What Cardano got right, what he got wrong, and why the book wasn’t published until a century after his death.
Chapter 14 — The Problem of Points
The Fermat-Pascal correspondence of 1654 — the founding moment of probability theory, born from a gambling question. The Chevalier de Méré’s dice problem. How to fairly split a pot in an interrupted game. Why this seemingly trivial question opened an entire universe of mathematics.
Chapter 15 — From Pascal to Bernoulli: Probability Takes Shape
Huygens’ De Ratiociniis in Ludo Aleae. Jacob Bernoulli’s Ars Conjectandi and the Law of Large Numbers. De Moivre’s Doctrine of Chances — written explicitly as a gambler’s manual. The normal distribution. How gambling problems drove the development of statistics for over a century.
Chapter 16 — Expected Value, the House Edge, and the Long Run
A self-contained chapter explaining the core mathematics that govern all gambling. Expected value, variance, standard deviation, gambler’s ruin, and the law of large numbers — made accessible to a general reader. Why the house always wins in the long run, and what “the long run” actually means. Kelly criterion. The mathematical difference between -EV entertainment and +EV speculation.

PART IV: GOD AND THE GAMBLER
Chapter 17 — Christianity and Gambling: Two Thousand Years of Ambivalence
Casting lots in the Bible — for Christ’s garments, for Matthias’s apostleship. The absence of an explicit Biblical prohibition. Augustine and Aquinas on gambling. The Puritan hostility. The Catholic relative tolerance. Methodism and the evangelical anti-gambling tradition. The modern American religious right and its campaigns against casinos. The current positions of every major Christian denomination.
Chapter 18 — Judaism and Gambling
The Talmudic discussion of mesahek b’kubya — the dice player as a disqualified witness. Maimonides on gambling. The Purim exception and festive gambling traditions. The nuanced Rabbinic position: gambling isn’t sinful per se, but the professional gambler is a kind of social parasite.
Chapter 19 — Islam: The Firmest Prohibition
A deeper treatment of Islamic anti-gambling theology. The specific Qur’anic verses. The distinction between gharar (uncertainty in contracts) and maysir (gambling). How Islamic finance navigates these boundaries. The complicated reality of gambling in modern Muslim-majority states — from the total prohibition in Saudi Arabia to the horse racing culture of the Gulf.
Chapter 20 — Hinduism, Buddhism, and the Dharmic Traditions
Hindu ambivalence — gambling is embedded in mythology and festival culture but cautioned against in ethical texts. The Buddhist explicit prohibition in the Sigalovada Sutta. Jain perspectives. How the karmic framework shapes attitudes toward chance and fortune. The paradox of Diwali gambling as a religious observance.
Chapter 21 — Confucianism, Taoism, and East Asian Moral Frameworks
Confucian disdain for gambling as unproductive and socially corrosive. Legalist attempts at prohibition in imperial China. The Taoist more relaxed posture toward fate and chance. The tension between official condemnation and pervasive cultural practice across East Asia.
Chapter 22 — Secular Morality and the Gambling Question
The Enlightenment reframing: gambling as a question of liberty, not sin. Utilitarian arguments for and against. Kantian objections. The libertarian case. The progressive case against gambling as exploitation of the poor. How the moral debate shifted from theology to economics and public health.

BOOK TWO: THE GAMES
A comprehensive treatment of every major form of gambling, its history, mechanics, mathematics, and culture

PART V: DICE AND TABLE GAMES
Chapter 23 — The Oldest Game: A History of Dice
From astragali to precision casino dice. The evolution of dice shapes, materials, and manufacturing. Loaded dice and the history of dice cheating. Hazard — the ancestor of craps — and its centuries-long run as Europe’s dominant dice game.
Chapter 24 — Craps: An American Reinvention
The African American origins of craps in 19th-century New Orleans. Bernard de Marigny. Street craps vs. casino craps. John H. Winn and the modern layout. The mathematics of every bet on the table. Why craps is simultaneously one of the best and worst bets in the casino.
Chapter 25 — Roulette: The Devil’s Wheel
Blaise Pascal’s alleged connection. The early history in French casinos. The single-zero European wheel vs. the double-zero American wheel — and how the Blanc brothers made Monte Carlo by offering better odds. The mathematics. Famous systems and why they all fail. The cultural mythology of roulette.
Chapter 26 — Blackjack: When the Player Fights Back
Vingt-et-un in French casinos. The evolution of rules. Edward Thorp’s Beat the Dealer (1962) and the birth of card counting. The Hi-Lo system. The MIT Blackjack Team. The casino countermeasures arms race. Ken Uston’s legal battles. The current state of the game and why 6:5 blackjack is a scandal.
Chapter 27 — Baccarat: The Biggest Game in the World
A game most Americans ignore but that generates more revenue globally than any other table game. Origins in Italy and France. Chemin de fer vs. punto banco. The Macau baccarat economy. The mathematics — the lowest house edge in the casino. Famous baccarat players and the Akio Kashiwagi story.
Chapter 28 — All the Other Table Games
A survey of casino table games past and present: Pai Gow, Pai Gow Poker, Sic Bo, Casino War, Three Card Poker (and the Derek Webb invention story), Let It Ride, Caribbean Stud, Spanish 21. How new casino games are invented, tested, and adopted — or abandoned. The economics of game licensing. Why most new casino games fail.

PART VI: LOTTERIES
Chapter 29 — The First Lotteries
Lottery-like games in the Roman Empire (the apophoreta). The Genoese lottery and its spread across Europe. The Dutch loterij — origin of the English word. The English state lottery from 1569 onward.
Chapter 30 — Lotteries and the Building of Nations
How lotteries financed the Virginia Company, the American colonies, and the early republic. Harvard, Yale, and Princeton partly built with lottery money. The 19th-century backlash and the Louisiana Lottery scandal — the most corrupt institution in American history. The complete prohibition era (1895–1964).
Chapter 31 — The Modern State Lottery
New Hampshire’s 1964 revival. The spread across America and the world. Scratch-offs, Powerball, Mega Millions. The mathematics of lottery design. The regressive-tax argument. Who actually plays and why. The international landscape: El Gordo, the UK National Lottery, and others. The cultural meaning of the jackpot.
Chapter 32 — Numbers Rackets and Illegal Lotteries
The policy game and the numbers racket in Black communities from the 1920s onward. Stephanie St. Clair, Bumpy Johnson, and the Harlem numbers runners. The economics and social role of illegal lotteries. How state lotteries effectively nationalized a Black-run underground industry.

PART VII: HORSE RACING AND ANIMAL CONTESTS
Chapter 33 — The Sport of Kings: Horse Racing from Antiquity
Racing in ancient Greece, Rome, and the Arab world. The founding of English racing — the Byerly Turk, the Darley Arabian, the Godolphin Barb, and the Thoroughbred breed. Newmarket and the Jockey Club. The cultural world of English racing.
Chapter 34 — American Racing and the Birth of the Bookie
Saratoga, Belmont, Churchill Downs. The golden age of American racing. The creation of bookmaking as a profession. The invention of pari-mutuel wagering by Pierre Oller. The totalisator machine. The racing wire services — from telegraph to telephone — and their control by organized crime.
Chapter 35 — The Global Racing World
Racing in Australia (Melbourne Cup as a national holiday), Japan (the largest racing economy on earth), Hong Kong (the Jockey Club as quasi-government), France, Ireland, Dubai, and beyond. Harness racing, greyhound racing, and the decline of both. The modern economics of racing and whether the sport can survive the competition from casinos and sports betting.
Chapter 36 — Cockfighting, Dog Fighting, and Animal Contest Gambling
An unflinching look at the oldest form of organized gambling entertainment. Cockfighting from ancient Greece to modern Southeast Asia and Latin America. The moral evolution that made these practices illegal in most of the West. Where they persist and why.

PART VIII: SPORTS BETTING
Chapter 37 — The Origins of Sports Wagering
Betting on the ancient Olympics. Prize fighting and pedestrianism in 18th- and 19th-century England. The development of organized sports betting alongside organized sports. Cricket betting scandals in the 1800s.
Chapter 38 — The Bookmaker’s Art
What a bookmaker actually does. How lines are set. The overround, vigorish, and the mathematics of bookmaking. Market-making vs. risk-taking. The evolution from individual bookmakers to syndicated operations.
Chapter 39 — The Point Spread and the American Sports Bet
Charles K. McNeil and the invention of the point spread in 1940s Chicago. Why the spread transformed sports betting from a niche activity into a mass phenomenon. The totals bet, the moneyline, and the parlay. The mathematics and psychology of each.
Chapter 40 — Fixing, Scandal, and Corruption
The Black Sox (1919). The CCNY basketball scandals (1951). The Boston College point-shaving case. Pete Rose. Tim Donaghy. Cricket match-fixing in South Asia. A systematic look at why sports betting creates corruption incentives and what has (and hasn’t) worked to combat it.
Chapter 41 — The Underground Market
Illegal sports betting in America from Prohibition through PASPA. The role of organized crime. Offshore books — Pinnacle, Bookmaker, 5Dimes, and the Costa Rica/Antigua/Curaçao ecosystem. Wiseguys, steam moves, and how the illegal market actually functioned as a sophisticated financial network.
Chapter 42 — The Legalization Explosion
PASPA’s passage in 1992 and repeal in 2018. Murphy v. NCAA. The state-by-state rollout. DraftKings, FanDuel, and the daily fantasy sports Trojan horse. The mobile revolution. How America went from prohibition to saturation in under five years. The European regulated model by comparison. The Australian betting culture and its cautionary lessons.
Chapter 43 — Sharps, Syndicates, and the Betting Market
The professional sports bettor — what the job actually looks like. Billy Walters. Haralabos Voulgaris. CRIS groups and betting syndicates. Closing line value as the only metric that matters. The efficient market hypothesis applied to sports betting. Why most people lose and the very few who don’t.

PART IX: MACHINES
Chapter 44 — The Slot Machine: Invention and Early History
Charles Fey and the Liberty Bell (1895). The early mechanical slots. The fruit machine in Britain. The role of organized crime in slot distribution. Bally and the electromechanical revolution.
Chapter 45 — The Digital Slot and the Psychology of Addiction
The transition to video slots. Random number generators. The Skinnerian design principles. Near-misses, losses disguised as wins, and the science of player engagement. Natasha Dow Schüll’s Addiction by Design and the argument that modern slots are engineered for compulsion. The slot machine as the most profitable product in the gambling industry.
Chapter 46 — Video Poker: The Thinking Person’s Machine
The development of video poker. Jacks or Better, Deuces Wild, and the full-pay variants. Why video poker is mathematically different from slots — some machines are beatable. The small community of professional video poker players.
Chapter 47 — Pachinko: Japan’s Hidden Gambling Empire
The history and mechanics of pachinko. The legal fiction (prizes, not cash — except at the window next door). A multi-billion-dollar industry largely invisible to the West. The Korean-Japanese social dynamics of the pachinko industry. Why pachinko matters for understanding gambling globally.

BOOK THREE: POKER
A dedicated history of poker from its murky origins to the present day

PART X: ORIGINS AND EARLY HISTORY
Chapter 48 — The Ancestors of Poker
Primero (16th-century Spain/Italy). Poque (France). Brag (England). Pochen (Germany). The As-Nas debate — David Parlett’s argument against Persian origins and the current scholarly consensus. What each ancestor contributed: betting structure, bluffing, hand rankings, community knowledge.
Chapter 49 — New Orleans and the Twenty-Card Game
The earliest documented references to poker in 1800s New Orleans. The 20-card, four-player game. The cultural milieu — French, Spanish, African, American. Jonathan H. Green’s 1843 account. Why New Orleans was the only city where this game could have been born.
Chapter 50 — Riverboats, Frontiers, and Sharpers
The Mississippi steamboat as a floating casino. Professional card sharps and their techniques — marked cards, cold decks, collusion. The expansion of poker westward with the frontier. The transition to the 52-card deck, the introduction of the flush and the straight, and the addition of the draw. The Civil War as a massive engine of poker diffusion.
Chapter 51 — Stud, Wild Cards, and the Proliferation of Variants
The development of stud poker during the Civil War. The introduction of wild cards and lowball. The community-card concept and its eventual triumph. A taxonomy of poker variants and their historical lineage. How each variant changed the strategic character of the game.
Chapter 52 — Poker in the American Imagination
Poker in literature — Mark Twain, Bret Harte, Owen Wister. Poker in film — from The Cincinnati Kid to Rounders. The game as a metaphor for American capitalism, meritocracy, and deception. Why poker became “the American game” and what that claim means.

PART XI: THE MODERN GAME TAKES SHAPE
Chapter 53 — Draw and Stud in the 20th Century
The poker landscape before Texas Hold’em’s dominance. Draw poker in home games and card rooms. Seven-card stud as the serious player’s game. The Gardena card rooms. The New York underground scene.
Chapter 54 — The Texas Road Gamblers
The oral history of high-stakes poker in mid-century Texas. Johnny Moss, Sailor Roberts, Amarillo Slim Preston, Doyle Brunson, Crandell Addington, Brian “Sailor” Roberts. Road trips, cheating, hijackings, and the mythology of the Texas road game. Separating fact from legend.
Chapter 55 — Benny Binion: From Dallas to Downtown
Binion’s biography — numbers runner, bootlegger, murderer, casino owner. The move from Dallas to Las Vegas. Binion’s Horseshoe and its unique character. The invention of the World Series of Poker (1970). The first Main Events and the crowning of Johnny Moss.
Chapter 56 — The World Series of Poker: Year by Year (1970–2002)
A detailed chronicle of every WSOP Main Event from the first through the pre-boom era. Key moments, iconic hands, the growth from invitation-only to open entry. Stu Ungar’s tragic genius. The WSOP as the institution that legitimized poker as a competitive pursuit.
Chapter 57 — Super/System and the Birth of Poker Literature
Doyle Brunson’s 1978 Super/System as a watershed. Why it was controversial. The Sklansky-Malmuth school — The Theory of Poker, Hold’em Poker for Advanced Players. Mike Caro’s Book of Tells. Dan Harrington’s tournament books. The arc of poker publishing from secret knowledge to mass education.
Chapter 58 — Hold’em Takes Over
How Texas Hold’em went from a Texas specialty to the dominant form of poker worldwide. The role of the WSOP, California card rooms, and television. The superiority of Hold’em as a spectator game. The marginalization of stud and other variants.

PART XII: THE BOOM AND ITS AFTERMATH
Chapter 59 — The Hole-Card Camera and the Television Revolution
Henry Orenstein’s 1995 patent. Late Night Poker in the UK. The World Poker Tour and its role in creating poker television. How a simple technological innovation made poker watchable.
Chapter 60 — Chris Moneymaker and the 2003 Main Event
A detailed account of the most consequential poker tournament ever played. Moneymaker’s $39 satellite, the final table, the Sam Farha bluff. Why this particular event ignited a global phenomenon. The “Moneymaker Effect” quantified.
Chapter 61 — The Online Poker Revolution
Planet Poker (1998), Paradise Poker, PartyPoker, PokerStars. The technology behind online poker. The economics of rake and player acquisition. How the internet created a poker economy orders of magnitude larger than anything before. The unprecedented hand volume and its effect on skill development. Multi-tabling. Screen names and online identities.
Chapter 62 — The Golden Age (2003–2011)
The peak years of the poker boom. The explosion of the WSOP. The WPT. High Stakes Poker and televised cash games. The online high-stakes ecosystem — Full Tilt’s “Rail Heaven,” the nosebleed games, and the cast of characters: Ivey, Dwan, Antonius, Galfond, Hansen. The culture of the era — excess, celebrity, and unprecedented money.
Chapter 63 — Cheating in the Online Era
The Absolute Poker/Ultimate Bet superuser scandals. Multi-accounting. Collusion. Bots. The challenge of ensuring integrity in a digital environment. How these scandals were discovered (often by the player community, not the sites).
Chapter 64 — Black Friday: April 15, 2011
The DOJ indictments. The seizure of PokerStars, Full Tilt, and Absolute Poker. The UIGEA backstory (2006). The Full Tilt insolvency revelation — player funds raided. The PokerStars acquisition of Full Tilt. The legal, political, and human aftermath. The overnight destruction of the American online poker ecosystem and the careers built within it.
Chapter 65 — Poker After Black Friday
The reshaping of the poker world. The European and global online market. Regulated online poker in Nevada, New Jersey, and beyond — too little, too late? The live tournament circuit boom. The shift in the poker economy’s center of gravity.

PART XIII: THE SCIENCE OF POKER
Chapter 66 — Von Neumann’s Game
John von Neumann chose poker as his model for strategic interaction. The minimax theorem (1928). Theory of Games and Economic Behavior (1944) and the simplified poker models within it. Why poker — not chess — was the game that gave birth to game theory.
Chapter 67 — Nash, Kuhn, and the Theory of Bluffing
Nash equilibrium and its application to poker. Kuhn poker as a teaching model. The mathematical structure of bluffing — why it’s not a trick but a necessity. The concept of an unexploitable strategy. Mixed strategies and randomization.
Chapter 68 — Solving Poker: From Toy Games to Hold’em
The computational challenge of solving poker. The game tree of NLHE and why brute force is impossible. Chris Ferguson’s early work. The University of Alberta poker research group and their decades-long project to solve various forms of poker.
Chapter 69 — CFR and the Solver Revolution
Counterfactual regret minimization explained from first principles. How the algorithm works, why it converges, and what it produces. The development of commercial solvers: PioSolver, MonkerSolver, Simple Postflop, GTO Wizard. How solvers transformed poker study from heuristic to computational. What a “GTO solution” actually is and what it isn’t.
Chapter 70 — Libratus, Pluribus, and Superhuman AI
The Carnegie Mellon milestones. Claudico (2015) — the failed first attempt. Libratus (2017) — beating heads-up professionals. Pluribus (2019) — beating six-handed. The technical innovations: nested subgame solving, blueprint strategies, depth-limited search. The philosophical implications for the game.
Chapter 71 — How the Modern Game Is Played
Range-based thinking. Frequencies and balance. Position, stack depth, and SPR. The GTO baseline with exploitative adjustments. How the vocabulary and conceptual framework of poker have been transformed. A snapshot of what high-level poker looks like in the 2020s, and why today’s average winning player would dominate the best players of previous eras.

BOOK FOUR: THE INDUSTRY
Casinos, regulation, organized crime, and the business of gambling

PART XIV: THE CASINO
Chapter 72 — The First Casinos: Venice to Baden-Baden
The Ridotto (Venice, 1638) — the first government-sanctioned gambling house. The spa town casinos of 18th- and 19th-century Europe. The Monte Carlo Casino and the Blanc family. What “casino” meant before Las Vegas redefined it.
Chapter 73 — The Invention of Las Vegas
The railroad town. The 1931 legalization of gambling in Nevada. The early casinos on Fremont Street. The El Rancho Vegas and the beginnings of the Strip. The desert as blank canvas for an entirely new kind of city.
Chapter 74 — The Mob Years
Bugsy Siegel and the Flamingo — fact vs. legend. Meyer Lansky’s financial architecture. The Cleveland, Chicago, and Kansas City mob families and their casino interests. The skim. How organized crime provided the capital that built Las Vegas when legitimate banks wouldn’t touch it.
Chapter 75 — The Corporate Takeover
Howard Hughes’s buying spree. The Nevada Corporate Gaming Act (1969). The rise of publicly traded casino companies. Steve Wynn and the reinvention of the Strip — the Mirage (1989) as inflection point. Sheldon Adelson, Kirk Kerkorian, and the megaresort era. How Las Vegas went from mob town to Fortune 500.
Chapter 76 — The Mathematics of Casino Management
Hold percentage, drop, win. How each game contributes to the casino’s bottom line. Table game vs. slot revenue mix. Comp theory and the reinvestment cycle. Yield management. The financial mechanics of running a casino, explained in detail.
Chapter 77 — Casino Design and Player Psychology
Bill Friedman’s maze layout vs. Roger Thomas’s open design. The sensory engineering of a casino floor — light, sound, temperature, oxygen. Why there are no clocks and no windows. Slot machine placement strategy. The Roger Thomas / Steve Wynn revolution in luxury casino design. Natasha Dow Schüll’s research on the “machine zone.”
Chapter 78 — Atlantic City: The Cautionary Tale
The 1976 legalization of casino gambling. Resorts International opens in 1978. The promise of urban renewal and the reality of what happened. The rise and decline and partial recovery. What Atlantic City teaches about using casinos as economic development tools.
Chapter 79 — Tribal Gaming and the Indian Casino
The Seminole bingo hall. California v. Cabazo Band (1987). The Indian Gaming Regulatory Act (1988). Foxwoods, Mohegan Sun, and the tribal casino economy. The enormous wealth generated — and the political and social complexities within tribal nations. The largest and least understood sector of American gambling.
Chapter 80 — Macau: The New Capital
The Portuguese colonial gambling monopoly. Stanley Ho and STDM. The 2002 liberalization and the entry of Wynn, Sands, and MGM. Macau surpassing Las Vegas in revenue by 2006. The VIP junket system. The Chinese anti-corruption crackdown under Xi Jinping and its devastating impact. The current landscape and future trajectory.
Chapter 81 — Global Casinos
A survey of the casino industry worldwide: Singapore’s Marina Bay Sands and the integrated resort model. Australia’s casino culture. South Korea’s foreigner-only casinos (and Kangwon Land for locals). The Philippines, Cambodia, and the emerging Southeast Asian casino market. European casinos. The Middle East’s conspicuous absence. Africa’s nascent industry.

PART XV: REGULATION, LAW, AND THE STATE
Chapter 82 — A History of Gambling Prohibition
Why governments ban gambling — morality, corruption, class, order. A cross-cultural survey of prohibition attempts from Roman law to modern times. The pattern that repeats everywhere: prohibition, underground flourishing, eventual legalization and taxation.
Chapter 83 — The American Regulatory Patchwork
The state-by-state legal landscape. Nevada’s regulatory model — the Gaming Control Board and the Nevada Gaming Commission. New Jersey’s different approach. The Wire Act, PASPA, UIGEA, and federal gambling law. The current state of online gambling legalization.
Chapter 84 — European Regulation
The UK Gambling Commission as a model. The Isle of Man, Malta, and Gibraltar as licensing jurisdictions. The Nordic state monopoly model. The tension between EU free-market principles and national gambling regulation. How Europe handles online gambling.
Chapter 85 — Gambling and Organized Crime
A sober, evidence-based assessment. Where organized crime genuinely controlled gambling and where the connection is overstated. The historical role of the mob in Las Vegas, Cuba, and elsewhere. Modern organized crime in Asian junket operations, illegal online gambling, and sports-fixing. How legalization and regulation have (and haven’t) displaced criminal involvement.
Chapter 86 — Gambling Taxation and Public Finance
How governments tax gambling — gross gaming revenue, turnover tax, player winnings tax. The economics of gambling as a revenue source. Whether gambling revenue is “new” or cannibalized from other spending. The public finance case for and against legal gambling.

BOOK FIVE: WIDER HORIZONS
The psychology, sociology, culture, and future of gambling

PART XVI: THE GAMBLER’S MIND
Chapter 87 — The Psychology of Gambling Behavior
Cognitive biases specific to gambling: the gambler’s fallacy, the hot hand fallacy, illusion of control, confirmation bias, sunk-cost reasoning. Prospect theory and loss aversion applied to gambling decisions. Why smart people make bad bets.
Chapter 88 — Problem Gambling and Addiction
Diagnostic criteria and prevalence rates. The neuroscience of gambling addiction — dopamine pathways and reward circuitry. Risk factors: genetics, personality, comorbidity. The gambling industry’s role and responsibility. Treatment approaches and their effectiveness. A chapter that takes the subject seriously without moralizing.
Chapter 89 — The Professional Gambler
A sociological portrait. What professional gambling actually looks like across domains — poker, sports betting, blackjack, horse racing, advantage play. The psychological profile, the lifestyle, the economics. Bankroll management as survival. The surprisingly small number of people who genuinely make a living gambling.
Chapter 90 — Women and Gambling
A chronically underexamined topic. Women in gambling history — from the Roman aleatoriae to modern poker. The gendering of gambling forms (slots as “feminized,” poker as “masculine”). The rise of women in professional poker. Gambling addiction patterns specific to women. An honest assessment of gambling as a heavily male-dominated world and why.

PART XVII: GAMBLING IN CULTURE
Chapter 91 — Gambling in Literature
Dostoevsky’s The Gambler (written to pay gambling debts). Pushkin’s The Queen of Spades. Damon Runyon’s Broadway. Ian Fleming’s Bond at the baccarat table. Hunter Thompson in Las Vegas. The literary treatment of gambling from romanticism to realism. Why gambling is irresistible to writers.
Chapter 92 — Gambling in Film and Television
The Sting, The Cincinnati Kid, Casino, Rounders, Molly’s Game, Uncut Gems. High Stakes Poker and the WPT. How screen depictions have shaped public perception of gambling and gamblers — mostly inaccurately. The particular challenge of making poker dramatic on screen.
Chapter 93 — Gambling in Art, Music, and Folklore
Caravaggio’s The Cardsharps. De La Tour’s candlelit card games. Cassius Coolidge’s dogs playing poker (and why it matters culturally despite being kitsch). Gambling songs from folk tradition through Kenny Rogers. Superstitions, rituals, and the folk beliefs of gamblers worldwide.

PART XVIII: FRONTIERS AND FUTURES
Chapter 94 — The Online Gambling Ecosystem
The current state of online gambling worldwide. Technology infrastructure — RNGs, live dealer games, mobile platforms. The regulatory map. The economics of customer acquisition and the unsustainable promotional environment. What the mature online gambling industry looks like.
Chapter 95 — Crypto Gambling and Decentralized Betting
Bitcoin casinos and provably fair gaming. Decentralized prediction markets (Augur, Polymarket). Smart contract-based wagering. The regulatory vacuum. The philosophical argument that cryptocurrency and gambling are natural partners — both are fundamentally about managing risk and uncertainty.
Chapter 96 — Prediction Markets, Information Markets, and the Expanding Definition
Where gambling ends and information markets begin. The Iowa Electronic Markets. The DARPA futures market controversy. Polymarket and the 2024 election. The argument that prediction markets are a public good and the counterargument that they’re just gambling with better PR.
Chapter 97 — Esports, Virtual Worlds, and the Next Frontier
Skin betting and the CS:GO scandal. Esports wagering. In-game economies and virtual item gambling. Loot boxes as slot machines. The blurring line between gaming and gambling. Regulatory responses and the generational shift in what “gambling” means.
Chapter 98 — Financial Markets as Gambling

That’s 99 chapters plus an epilogue, across five books and eighteen parts. The structure is designed so that each Book works as a standalone volume (you could publish them separately), but together they form a single coherent narrative that moves from the deepest human roots of gambling through every game, every culture, every legal regime, and every moral argument — arriving at the present day and looking forward.
The poker material gets its own full Book (Three) because the depth of that history justifies it, and because a poker player picking this up would want to find that material as a self-contained unit.
The thing that makes this different from David Schwartz’s Roll the Bones (the closest existing single-volume attempt) is the ambition to be simultaneously readable and encyclopedic — you should be able to look up “What does Islam say about gambling?” or “When was the point spread invented?” or “How does counterfactual regret minimization work?” and find a thorough, well-sourced answer, but you should also be able to read it cover to cover as a story about humanity’s relationship with chance.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
